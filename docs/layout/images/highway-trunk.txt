Title: Port Merging — Merged Trunk vs Individual Slots

Purpose:
  Show why a merged trunk is cleaner than individual port slots.
  Left panel: 5 connections each using their own port slot — stubs cross in the
  stub area, messy. Right panel: same 5 connections using ONE merged trunk — a comb
  fan-out in the stub area is clean and non-crossing.

Left panel — "Individual port slots (messy)":
  One block on the left ("Hub") with 5 port stubs on its right face, spaced at
  irregular y-positions (not at the lanes they need). Five destination blocks on
  the right column at different y-positions. The stubs from the Hub cross each other
  between the Hub face and the corridor, creating a tangle before the wires even
  enter the highway. Arrow at top: "5 individual slots — stubs cross at entry".

Right panel — "Merged trunk (clean)":
  Same Hub block on the left, same 5 destination blocks on the right.
  Hub has ONE port on its right face (a single merged stub, drawn as a thick line).
  The thick trunk runs from the Hub face to the corridor left wall.
  In the stub area (between Hub face and corridor), a COMB structure fans the single
  trunk into 5 parallel wires at equal G spacing. The comb looks like a short vertical
  spine with 5 horizontal teeth branching off. Inside the corridor, 5 parallel
  horizontal wires run cleanly. At the far side, another comb fans the wires back to
  individual port stubs entering the destination blocks.
  Arrow at top: "1 merged trunk — comb fan-out is non-crossing".

Key annotations:
  Left: red callout "stubs cross!" near the crossing region in the stub area.
  Right: label "merged trunk" on the thick stub from Hub face.
         label "comb fan-out" on the stub-area comb.
         label "parallel lanes" inside the corridor.
  Bottom: note "PortAssigner groups by (face, directionality, highway_id) → one trunk per group"

Key message:
  The merged trunk moves all complexity from the block face (where N slots would be
  needed) into the stub area (where a comb is a non-crossing structure). The block
  face needs only 1 port point regardless of N. The stub area minimum length is
  ceil((N-1)/2) × G to allow the comb to fan out without wire crossings.
