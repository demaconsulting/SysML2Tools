Title: Back-Edge Arc Routing — Loop-Back Transitions Outside the Flow Column

Purpose:
  Show how back-edges (transitions from a lower layer to an upper layer) are routed as
  arcs around the outside of the main flow column, at increasing radii for nested loops.
  This is the State Transition View and Action Flow View pattern.

Layout:
  A vertical flow column of four state boxes, top to bottom:
    State A (top, Layer 0) — initial pseudo-state (filled circle) above it
    State B (Layer 1)
    State C (Layer 2)
    State D (bottom, Layer 3)

  Forward transitions (solid arrows with arrowheads pointing down):
    A → B
    B → C
    C → D

  Back-edge 1 (outer arc, nesting depth 0):
    D → B: a curved arc that exits State D on the right, travels to the right of the
    column, arcs upward, and re-enters State B from the right.
    Arc radius R_0 = EdgeClearance × 1.
    Labelled "back-edge depth 0  R = EdgeClearance".

  Back-edge 2 (deeper arc, nesting depth 1):
    C → A: a curved arc that exits State C on the right, travels further to the right
    (outside Back-edge 1's arc), arcs upward past State B, and re-enters State A.
    Arc radius R_1 = EdgeClearance × 2.
    Labelled "back-edge depth 1  R = 2·EdgeClearance".

  The two arcs are drawn concentrically on the right side of the column — the deeper
  one (C→A) is visibly further from the column than the shallower one (D→B). They
  do not cross each other.

  A small legend or bracket on the right shows the two radii with arrows.

Key message:
  Treating concurrent back-edges as nested intervals (like nested parentheses) and
  assigning radius R_k = EdgeClearance × (1 + k) guarantees no two back-edge arcs
  ever overlap or cross, regardless of how many nested loops exist.
